1. Opening
Literary allusion or dramatic scene Never the thesis. Opens with a Chesterton quote, a Yiddish proverb, a wordplay neologism, or a vivid anecdote. The hook establishes the frame before the argument begins. "G. K. Chesterton's novel The Flying Inn begins with a strange seaside encounter…"
Stark claim or data barrage Often the thesis itself. Opens with a single blunt sentence or a cascade of statistics. The reader knows the argument within 30 words. "Britain is ungoverned." / "THE WORLD is rearming fast. Military spending has increased every year for a decade."
First-person scene or provocative thesis The writer in a situation, or the thesis stated baldly in the title and first sentence. Personal experience substitutes for the hook. "I remember screaming, though I don't know what words I screamed." / "Tim Cook's job was to make Apple boring—and he did."
2. Thesis
Buried — paragraph 2–4 Always preceded by a concession to the opposing view. The "yes, but" structure is a defining feature: grant the best version of the other side, then pivot. "Profound dread is a reasonable reaction… But it is chiefly these fears which we may, as Christians, find relief."
Within first 5 sentences Typically introduced by a single adversative word — "Yet," "But," "Still" — that flips the opening setup. Concession is brief, often just one clause. "Yet although the outlook for Democrats is good, for America's democracy it is alarming."
Title and sentence 1 (Ideas) / Embedded (Magazine) Opinion pieces state the thesis in the headline, then repeat it immediately. Magazine features withhold it entirely, letting the argument emerge through narrative. "Apple Is Boring Now" → "Tim Cook's job was to make Apple boring—and he did."
3. Evidence
Named authority + historical genealogy Cites named scholars, Church Fathers, or classical texts by name in every article. Traces precedent across centuries. Data and statistics are almost entirely absent. Bellarmine, Newman, Anscombe, Psalm 139, Acts 2, 1 Corinthians 15:17
Data + named individuals as types Precise statistics in every article. Named real people (Thom Tillis, Peter Mandelson) serve as typological cases for structural arguments, not as authorities. "European NATO members will spend an additional €300bn per year by end of decade."
Personal testimony + selective data The writer's own experience is primary evidence. Data appears but less precisely. Named individuals are people the writer has met or observed directly. "I run weekly focus groups… 'Marco Rubio, I think, is an amazing dude,' said Ken, a Biden 2020/Trump 2024 voter from Georgia."
4. Escalation
Always — to civilizational or theological stakes Every article zooms out before the close. A debate about just war becomes a debate about what Christianity is. A book review becomes a warning about Western self-understanding.
Rare — stays bounded to the policy question Arguments stay at the level they started. A leader on Israel's military doctrine does not become a meditation on Western civilisation. The Economist considers this a virtue.
Occasional — implied, not announced Some pieces reach toward a broader implication in the closing paragraph, but it is understated rather than declared. The Atlantic does not preach. "Their absence can't be blamed on China, Russia, or Iran. Maybe the White House should look into it."
5. Close
Prophetic — invokes a historical standard 1–3 paragraphs. Prescriptive or urgent. Often names a historical figure (Thomas More, Newman, Pope Leo I) whose example the present fails to meet. "We are not asked to be unafraid. We are asked to believe that the one who says 'Do not be afraid' has earned the right to say it."
Single "kicker" sentence, then ■ One ironic or judicial sentence — never a summary. Crystallises the argument into its sharpest form and stops. The ■ is the full stop. "A superpower in rule-making, yet a supplicant in everything else that matters. ■" / "Britain is not ungovernable. Even so, Sir Keir cannot govern. ■"
Emotional image or echoed thesis Returns to the opening scene, or restates the thesis once more — plainly, without fanfare. Magazine features close like short stories. "Tim Cook saw to it, and now somebody else will." / "He woke early one morning, agitated and coughing, calling out, 'Elisabeth?' I was there. But then, in a blink, he wasn't."
6. Voice
Named intellectual Writer is identified. Authority derives from command of the tradition. Occasional first person, but always deferring outward to a larger source.
Anonymous institution "I" never appears. The Economist speaks as a corporate entity. Authority derives from observable reality and data, not the individual writer's identity or experience.
Named writer as witness "I" appears in 7 of 10 articles. Authority derives from direct experience. The writer's credentials are the story they were present for, not the books they have read.

In short
First Things

Argues from tradition. The most persuasive authority is external and historical. Every piece escalates to civilizational stakes. Closes like a sermon.

The Economist

Argues from evidence. The writer disappears; the institution speaks. Arguments stay bounded. Closes like a verdict.

The Atlantic

Argues from experience. The writer's witnessed life is the primary credential. Closes like the last line of a short story.
