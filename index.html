How three magazines argue

A structural comparison of First Things, The Economist, and The Atlantic

In short
First Things

Argues from tradition. Authority is external and historical. Every piece escalates to civilisational stakes. Closes like a sermon.

The Economist

Argues from evidence. The writer disappears; the institution speaks. Arguments stay bounded. Closes like a verdict.

The Atlantic

Argues from experience. The writer's witnessed life is the primary credential. Closes like the last line of a short story.

Argument structure
First Things 7-phase · avg. 2,460 words · named scholar in every article
1. Hook
Literary allusion, dramatic scene, or wordplay — never the thesis Opens with a Chesterton quotation, a Yiddish proverb, a coined neologism, or a vivid historical anecdote. Establishes the frame before the argument begins. "G. K. Chesterton's novel The Flying Inn begins with a strange seaside encounter…"
2. Concession
Grant the strongest version of the opposing view Appears in 7 of 10 articles. Establishes intellectual fairness before the attack. The reader is positioned as a reasonable person who might initially disagree.
3. Pivot
"But actually…" — the real thesis, buried at paragraph 2–4 The "yes, but" structure is the defining move. Concede, then counter. The thesis arrives late and with force. "Profound dread is a reasonable reaction… But it is chiefly these fears which we may, as Christians, find relief."
4. Genealogy
Named scholarly authority + historical precedent across centuries Named scholars, Church Fathers, or classical texts in every article. Data and statistics almost entirely absent. Evidence is textual and historical, not empirical. Bellarmine, Newman, Anscombe, Psalm 139, Acts 2, 1 Corinthians 15:17
5. Application
How the historical case illuminates the present The recovered tradition is aimed at a contemporary problem — a policy question, a political figure, a cultural crisis.
6. Escalation
Zoom out to civilisational or theological stakes — always Every article widens before the close. A debate about just war becomes a question about what Christianity is. A book review becomes a warning about Western self-understanding.
7. Close
Prophetic — 1–3 paragraphs, invoking a historical standard Prescriptive or urgent. Often names a historical figure whose example the present fails to meet. Closes like a sermon. "We are not asked to be unafraid. We are asked to believe that the one who says 'Do not be afraid' has earned the right to say it."
The Economist 5-phase · avg. 902 words · data in every article · no byline
1. Claim
Stark declarative sentence or data barrage — often the thesis itself The reader knows the argument within 30 words. No extended hook. No scene-setting for its own sake. "Britain is ungoverned." / "THE WORLD is rearming fast. Military spending has increased every year for a decade."
2. Pivot
One adversative word flips the opening — within the first 5 sentences "Yet," "But," or "Still" introduces the core tension immediately. Concession is a clause, not a paragraph. "Yet although the outlook for Democrats is good, for America's democracy it is alarming."
3. Evidence
Precise data + named individuals as typological cases Statistics appear in every article, often with exact figures and dates. Named real people (Thom Tillis, Peter Mandelson) illustrate structural arguments — they are cases, not authorities. "European NATO members will spend an additional €300bn per year by end of decade."
4. Prescription
What should be done — 1–2 paragraphs, stays bounded to the specific question Arguments do not escalate to civilisational stakes. A leader on Israel's military doctrine does not become a meditation on Western decline. The Economist considers this a virtue.
5. Kicker ■
One ironic or judicial sentence, then ■ — never a summary Crystallises the argument into its sharpest form and stops. The ■ is the full stop. This is the most structurally distinctive feature of Economist writing. "A superpower in rule-making, yet a supplicant in everything else that matters. ■" / "Britain is not ungovernable. Even so, Sir Keir cannot govern. ■"
The Atlantic Two templates (Ideas vs. Magazine) · "I" in 7 of 10 articles · bimodal length
1. Opening
First-person scene (Ideas) or thesis in title + sentence 1 (Opinion) Ideas pieces state the thesis in the headline and repeat it immediately. Magazine features open inside an experience — no thesis declared. "I remember screaming, though I don't know what words I screamed." / "Tim Cook's job was to make Apple boring—and he did."
2. Authority
Personal credential established — the writer's experience is the evidence "I run weekly focus groups." "I had been working in Iraq for a decade." "My husband refused to go to the ER." The "I" is structural, not stylistic — it legitimises the claim.
3. Evidence
Personal testimony + named individuals the writer has met directly Data appears but less precisely than the Economist. Named sources are people observed first-hand, not scholars or historical figures. "'Marco Rubio, I think, is an amazing dude,' said Ken, a Biden 2020/Trump 2024 voter from Georgia."
4. Diagnosis
The personal case reveals a structural or systemic problem One person's experience becomes evidence for a broader claim about hospitals, institutions, or politics. The move from particular to general is shown, not announced.
5. Close
Emotional image or echoed thesis — closes like a short story, not a verdict Returns to the opening scene, or restates the thesis plainly without fanfare. Escalation is occasional and understated. The Atlantic does not preach. "Tim Cook saw to it, and now somebody else will." / "He woke early one morning, calling out, 'Elisabeth?' I was there. But then, in a blink, he wasn't."
